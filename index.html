The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra
Intro Monomials Polynomials The Proof Summary

Every polynomial has a root — and you can see why

This page builds up, in three visual stages, the classic winding-number proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. Each stage is animated, so you can watch the argument happen instead of just reading it. The formal equations are always one click away, but you shouldn't need them to follow the idea.

Prerequisite: comfort with complex numbers in the form \(z = re^{it}\). No topology required — we'll build the one topological idea we need (winding number) from scratch.

1. Complex monomials: \(z^k = r^k e^{ikt}\)

Start with the simplest possible building block: a single power of \(z\). Write the input in polar form,

\[ z = r e^{it}. \]

Here \(r\) is fixed and controls the radius of the circle the input travels around. The variable \(t\) is time — the angle swept out as the input goes once around that circle, from \(t=0\) to \(t=2\pi\). When we compare several monomials, we always feed them the same \(t\) at the same moment, so we can compare how fast each one rotates.

Raising \(z\) to the power \(k\) does two things at once:

  • It raises the radius to the power \(k\): the output travels on a circle of radius \(r^k\).
  • It multiplies the angle by \(k\): \(z^k = r^k e^{i(kt)}\).

So as the input \(z\) travels once around its circle, the output \(z^k\) travels \(k\) times around the origin. A higher power means a higher angular velocity — the output point spins faster.

Takeaway: when \(t\) increases at the same rate for every monomial, the vector \(z^k\) rotates \(k\) times as fast as \(z\) itself. The exponent controls both the output radius (\(r^k\)) and the number of rotations (\(k\)). Keep this one fact in mind — it is the entire engine behind the proof in Section 3.

2. Polynomials as sums of rotating vectors

Now build a general polynomial out of these monomials:

\[ p(z) = a_0 + a_1 z + a_2 z^2 + \cdots + a_n z^n. \]

Each term \(a_k z^k\) is one rotating vector, spinning at its own angular velocity \(k\), as we just saw. To visualize the sum, draw the vectors head to tail:

  1. Start at the origin and draw the constant vector \(a_0\).
  2. From its tip, attach the vector \(a_1 z\).
  3. From that tip, attach \(a_2 z^2\).
  4. Continue through the final term \(a_n z^n\).

The vector from the origin to the final tip is the value \(p(z)\). This is exactly ordinary vector addition — we are not composing or concatenating the monomial functions, only adding the complex numbers they produce at a shared instant \(t\).

Every one of these vectors is evaluated at the same \(z = re^{it}\). They rotate at different speeds because their powers differ, and their head-to-tail sum is the polynomial's output. As \(t\) sweeps from \(0\) to \(2\pi\), that final endpoint traces a closed curve — the image of the input circle under \(p\).

\[ p(re^{it}) = a_0 + a_1 r e^{it} + a_2 r^2 e^{2it} + \cdots + a_n r^n e^{int}. \]

Takeaway: a polynomial curve is a sum of circles spinning at different speeds. Small radii keep every term small except \(a_0\); large radii let the highest power dominate. That contrast between small and large \(r\) is exactly what the proof in Section 3 uses.

3. The winding-number proof

Theorem. Every nonconstant complex polynomial has at least one complex root. Consequently, a degree-\(n\) complex polynomial has exactly \(n\) roots, counted with multiplicity.

Let

\[ p(z) = a_0 + a_1 z + \cdots + a_n z^n, \qquad a_n \neq 0. \]

For each radius \(r > 0\), consider the closed curve traced by the polynomial on the circle of radius \(r\):

\[ \gamma_r(t) = p(re^{it}), \qquad 0 \le t \le 2\pi. \]

The central animation below shows one such curve. Use the buttons to reveal the proof one stage at a time.

Winding number, informally: the winding number of a closed curve around the origin is the (signed) number of times the curve loops around the origin as you trace it once. It is always a whole number for a curve that never touches the origin.

When \(r\) is very small, every term except the constant is tiny, so

\[ p(re^{it}) \approx a_0. \]

The curve is only a small deformation of the fixed point \(a_0\). Assuming \(a_0 \neq 0\), this tiny curve stays clustered near \(a_0\) and never gets close enough to the origin to wind around it. Its winding number around \(0\) is therefore \(0\).

Edge case: if \(a_0 = 0\), we are already done — \(z = 0\) is a root of \(p\), and the theorem holds trivially.

Now let \(r\) grow continuously from small to large. Because \(p\) is a polynomial — and polynomials are continuous — the curve \(\gamma_r\) changes continuously as a function of both \(r\) and \(t\). There are no sudden jumps: a small change in \(r\) produces only a small change in the curve. Look back at the Section 2 animation: it shows exactly this kind of continuous change in shape as \(r\) increases across four radii.

For sufficiently large \(r\), the highest-power term dominates every other term:

\[ p(re^{it}) \approx a_n r^n e^{int}. \]

This leading term travels around the origin exactly \(n\) times as \(t\) runs from \(0\) to \(2\pi\) — that is Section 1's rule, \(z^n\) winds \(n\) times as fast as \(z\). So for a sufficiently large circle, the polynomial curve has winding number \(n\) around the origin.

Why do the lower-degree terms become insignificant? Compare any term \(k < n\) to the leading term:

\[ \frac{|a_k| r^k}{|a_n| r^n} = \frac{|a_k|}{|a_n|\, r^{\,n-k}} \longrightarrow 0 \quad \text{as } r \to \infty. \]

Every smaller power shrinks to nothing next to \(a_n r^n\) once \(r\) is large enough.

Here is the one topological fact the whole proof rests on:

A closed curve can change its winding number around the origin only by passing through the origin.

This should feel plausible: the winding number is an integer count of loops. To change from one whole number of loops to another, the curve has to cross over the point it's winding around — otherwise the "loop count" can't tick up or down.

Now suppose, for contradiction, that \(p\) has no root. Then

\[ p(re^{it}) \neq 0 \quad \text{for every } r > 0 \text{ and every } t. \]

The curves \(\gamma_r\) would then deform continuously from the small-radius curve to the large-radius curve without ever touching the origin. By the fact above, their winding number would have to stay exactly the same the whole time.

But we just showed the winding number starts at \(0\) (Stage A) and ends at \(n\) (Stage C). If \(p\) is nonconstant, \(n \geq 1\), so \(0 \neq n\) — the winding number is not the same at both ends. Contradiction.

Therefore the assumption was false: there must exist some radius \(r\) and angle \(t\) with

\[ p(re^{it}) = 0. \]

The polynomial has at least one complex root.

The winding argument above proves existence of a single root — it does not say the curve crosses the origin \(n\) separate times, and we will not claim that.

Instead, the rest of the count follows by factoring:

  • Stage D shows every nonconstant complex polynomial has at least one root \(\alpha\).
  • Once \(p(\alpha) = 0\), polynomial division gives \(p(z) = (z - \alpha)\, q(z)\), where \(q\) has degree \(n-1\).
  • If \(q\) is nonconstant, apply Stage D again to \(q\) to find another root.
  • Repeating this \(n\) times peels off \(n\) linear factors, giving \(n\) roots counted with multiplicity.

Advanced note (argument principle). A stronger statement is true: the winding number of \(\gamma_r\) equals the number of zeros of \(p\) lying strictly inside the circle of radius \(r\), counted with multiplicity — provided \(\gamma_r\) never passes through the origin. This is a special case of the argument principle from complex analysis, and it is what ultimately justifies counting "\(n\) roots," not just "at least one."

Formal statement

Let \(p(z) = \sum_{k=0}^n a_k z^k\) with \(a_n \neq 0\) and \(n \geq 1\). For \(r > 0\) define \(\gamma_r : [0, 2\pi] \to \mathbb{C}\) by \(\gamma_r(t) = p(re^{it})\).

If \(\gamma_r\) never passes through \(0\), its winding number \(w(r) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \big(\arg \gamma_r(2\pi) - \arg \gamma_r(0)\big)\) (with continuous branch of \(\arg\) along the curve) is a well-defined integer, and \(w(r)\) is locally constant in \(r\) wherever \(\gamma_r\) avoids \(0\), since winding number is a continuous integer-valued function of continuously varying non-vanishing loops.

As \(r \to 0^+\), \(\gamma_r \to a_0\) uniformly, so for small enough \(r\) (assuming \(a_0 \neq 0\)), \(\gamma_r\) is homotopic to the constant loop at \(a_0\) within \(\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}\), giving \(w(r) = 0\).

As \(r \to \infty\), \(\big\| \gamma_r(t) - a_n r^n e^{int} \big\| = O(r^{n-1})\) while \(|a_n r^n e^{int}| = |a_n| r^n\), so for sufficiently large \(r\), \(\gamma_r\) is homotopic (within \(\mathbb{C}\setminus\{0\}\)) to \(t \mapsto a_n r^n e^{int}\), which has winding number \(n\).

If \(p\) had no zero anywhere, \(\gamma_r(t) \neq 0\) for all \(r, t\), so \(w(r)\) would be constant for all \(r > 0\) — contradicting \(w(r) \to 0\) as \(r \to 0^+\) and \(w(r) \to n\) as \(r \to \infty\). Hence \(p\) has a zero \(\alpha \in \mathbb{C}\).

Writing \(p(z) = (z-\alpha) q(z)\) with \(\deg q = n - 1\) and inducting on degree gives \(n\) zeros of \(p\), counted with multiplicity. \(\blacksquare\)

Proof summary

  1. Near \(r = 0\), the constant term dominates, so the winding number is \(0\).
  2. For large \(r\), the leading term dominates, so the winding number is \(n\).
  3. The winding number cannot change during a continuous deformation unless the curve passes through the origin.
  4. Therefore \(p(z) = 0\) for some complex number \(z\).
  5. Factor out that root and repeat to obtain \(n\) roots, counted with multiplicity.
